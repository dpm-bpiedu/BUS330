Glossary

access to courts The rights to petition to address grievances, have access to attorneys, and have access to legal materials and other legal assistance.
administration The process of managing a correctional (or any) organization.
argot roles Roles assumed by prisoners as a result of their incarceration.
authority The legitimate power inherent in a position to manage, sanction, and provide incentives.
banishment In colonial America, settlement laws used to keep people from entering the community or to remove them from it.
benefit of clergy A practice dating back to the Middle Ages that exempted clergy from certain civil punishments.
Big House Early 20th-century maximum security prisons, characterized by tight security and emphasis on custody and warehousing of inmates.
bureaucracy An organizational form characterized by hierarchy of authority, written rules and procedures, specialization, and expertise.
capital punishment The death penalty.
classification The separation of prisoners into categories based on a diagnostic analysis of an individual's personal history and treatment needs, which resulted in minimum, medium, and maximum security distinctions for prisons.
Coker v. Georgia (1977) The Supreme Court case that established that the death penalty cannot be sought for rape crimes.
collateral visit When a probation officer visits those affiliated with a probationer.
community corrections The array of programs found in the community to address crime and offenders.
community service Work, as ordered by the court, that offenders perform as payment for their crimes.
community supervision The use of the community as a resource to manage offenders. Probation and parole are the most visible elements of community supervision.
congregate system A penitentiary system developed in Auburn, New York, in which prisoners slept alone in individual cells, labored together during the day, marched in a lockstep shuffle, and ate their meals in central dining halls but were forbidden to communicate with each other.
consensual model A model of prison management that stresses inmate participation.
consent decree A settlement of a lawsuit in which the administration agrees to take certain actions without admitting fault or guilt for the situation that gave rise to the lawsuit.
constitutional law The guarantees and protections afforded under the U.S. Constitution and state constitutions, and the court decisions regarding their application.
control model A model of prison management that stresses security and prisoner discipline.
convict subculture A group of prisoners who believe in the inmate code because of their lengthy incarceration.
corporal punishment Any punishment, such as whipping, that causes pain to the body.
correctional aims The varied purposes that corrections is attempting to achieve.
correctional menu The correctional strategies used in the United States.
crisis intervention team (CIT) A team of jail employees or law enforcement personnel trained to address mental health problems among offenders in jails.
death penalty The ultimate sentence, which requires the taking of an offender's life.
decision-making guidelines Suggested decisions based on the development of weighted factors that concern (a) the offense committed and its specific circumstances and (b) the offender and factors specific to his or her personal history.
degradation A process by which the inmate is demeaned as a result of the jailing process.
Department of Corrections (or Division of Corrections) A state or federal agency designated by law to manage correctional populations, which can include both incarcerated offenders and those managed in the community.
deterrence Actions designed to send a message to the offender and society that criminal offending is not acceptable.
differential attachment Varied responses to the adoption of the inmate code, depending on the part of the prison sentence being served.
discretionary release The form of conditional release for which offenders have gone before a parole board or commission.
disintegration A process by which the inmate loses all ties to the external world as a result of the jailing process.
disorientation A process by which the inmate loses his or her identity and sense of self as a result of the jailing process.
diversion Programs or correctional efforts in which the accused agrees to participate in order to avoid being criminally prosecuted for an alleged offense.
"don't hurt me" agenda An administrative strategy that advocates making as few waves as possible and appeasing any significantly competing interests.
due process The opportunity for someone accused of a crime to defend him- or herself before punishment can occur.
electronic monitoring The use of surveillance mechanisms to monitor the location and movement of people in the criminal justice system.
equal protection of the laws The 14th Amendment provides a guarantee of equal protection to groups of citizens within American society.
evolving oversight era The current period in which the courts (especially the federal courts) aim to balance prisoners' rights with the government's interest in carrying out legitimate correctional facility aims.
external environment The significant forces outside the organization that affect its operation.
Felker v. Turpin (1996) The Supreme Court case that established that the 1996 Antiterrorism and Effective Death Penalty Act did not diminish the writ of habeas corpus.
filing of cases A suspension of action against an offender in the event his or her behavior becomes more law abiding.
fine Money paid to the court for violating the law.
forecasting The use of statistical and methodological techniques to make predictions.
formal organization The explicit aspects of an organization, including its rules and policies, structure, positions, authority, and channels of communication.
functional/deprivation model A model of inmate social systems that lists and describes the experiences of prisoners and how they adapt to the prison environment.
furlough A policy that allows inmates to leave their places of confinement and go into the community for a short period, usually for a few days and usually unsupervised.
Furman v. Georgia (1972) The Supreme Court case that established that the death penalty can be ruled unconstitutional if it inflicts undue pain and suffering. As practiced at the time, the death penalty was held to be unconstitutional, but it was left up to Congress and state legislatures to determine methods of execution that would not inflict undue harm or suffering.
general conditions of probation A set of conditions in a specific jurisdiction that all probationers must follow.
generalized caseloads A caseload comprising disparate numbers and types of probationers and offenses.
Gregg v. Georgia (1976) The Supreme Court case that ruled mandatory death sentences are unconstitutional and created a bifurcated system for assessing aggravating and mitigating circumstances to determine whether an offender should receive the death penalty.
habeas corpus Latin for "you have the body," meaning a person is in custody; a judicial order to bring a detained person to court, which can allow him or her to be released from confinement in the event of a constitutional violation.
halfway houses Residential facilities that usually provide more structure and a greater degree of supervision for court-ordered residents than do other community corrections endeavors.
hands-off era The period during which the courts (especially the federal courts) minimally intervened in corrections and took a deferential position vis-à-vis prison administrators.
hands-on era The period during which the courts (especially the federal courts) took an activist position toward corrections in the United States, intervening to examine correctional policies and practices to ensure appropriate attention was paid to prisoners' rights.
hard time A period of difficulties or hardship.
house arrest A sentence imposed by the court under which offenders must remain confined in their own residences.
image management The process of designing and controlling an organization's representation.
importation model A view of the prison social structure that suggests not only that the mechanisms of adaptation to prison are learned prior to incarceration, but that these modes of adaptation can be tied directly to specific role types. These role types make up three referent subcultures: the thief subculture, convict subculture, and legitimate subculture.
incapacitation The imposition of a restraint on an offender such that criminal wrongdoing is not possible (e.g., imprisonment).
incarceration The use of a correctional facility (e.g., jail or prison) to house criminal offenders.
informal organization Patterns of behavior, interaction, and power that arise from personal association in an organization, beyond the explicit formal arrangements.
in-reach Programming intended for correctional populations while they are still incarcerated and just prior to their release.
intensive probation Any conditions that monitor a probationer more intensely.
interest groups Organized groups of people who want their preferences expressed in policies or actions—in this case, those that pertain to corrections organizations.
internal environment Elements within an organization that shape its policies and actions.
Jacksonian era An era in prison history when institutions, like jails, were viewed as the appropriate societal response to crime and deviancy.
judicial reprieve A judicial practice used to delay the imposition of a criminal sentence based on an offender's good behavior.
Justice System Improvement Act Also known as the Percy Amendment, a federal legislation that enabled states to produce prison made goods and sell them across state lines. Prior to this federal legislative change, states could only sell prison made goods within their states' borders.
lease/purchase contracts Arrangements whereby private groups build and finance correctional facilities and lease them to governmental entities, with the intention of eventually selling them to those entities.
legitimate subculture The group of inmates who hold legitimate values as expressed in society.
linear/intermittent surveillance A design and philosophy that stresses minimum prisoner–staff contact and minimal supervision.
mandatory release A type of release from prison whereby an offender is placed on community supervision as a matter of legislation, rather than because of the actions of a parole board or commission.
McCleskey v. Kemp (1987) The Supreme Court case that established that statistical data is not sufficient to show the death penalty is unfair and arbitrary to one racial group compared to another.
niches Small groups of prisoners who band together to assist one another during their imprisonment.
overprediction A term used to describe when a paroling authority tries to reduce the potential for false negatives obtaining parole and in doing so denies parole to an unknown number of false positives, who would not have committed a violent act but cannot demonstrate that fact.
pains of imprisonment The psychological difficulties experienced by prisoners due to their incarceration.
parole An administrative release decision by a correctional authority to allow an offender to be placed in the community during the latter part of his or her sentence under general and specific conditions.
penitentiary A facility in which inmates were to repent for their acts and reform; separation was encouraged.
personal visit A visit from the probation officer to the probationer.
podular/direct supervision A design and philosophy of maximum interaction between prisoners and staff within a housing unit.
podular/remote surveillance A design and philosophy of limited and controlled interaction between prisoners and staff in a housing unit.
policy A formally expressed practice or strategy of action.
policy innovation The creation of new approaches to address new problems.
policy succession The process by which "new" policies grow from previous ones and substantially reflect attempts to "solve" the same problems or those created by previous policies.
political resultants A term used by Allison (1971) to refer to decisions resulting from bargaining among key decision makers.
postsentence investigation An additional service provided by probation agencies to support the offender while on probation and improve decision making regarding his or her situation.
power The ability to influence or obtain compliance either as a result of formal authority or the development of informal influence.
preparation A process by which the inmate creates a self-identity that incorporates the criminal lifestyle as a result of the jailing process.
presentence investigation A formal report that documents the circumstances and factors in a probationer's life. The report helps the court determine an appropriate sentence.
pretrial detainees Individuals who are in custody awaiting a criminal trial and have not been convicted.
pretrial detention The detention of offenders in the jail prior to an actual trial.
pretrial release The release of a person awaiting trial into the community, rather than incarcerating him or her in jail.
prisonization A process of adaptation to the prison by prisoners.
private agenda A belief among correctional officers that their role is to help prisoners adjust to the prison setting.
private culture Beliefs held by prisoners that support adjustment and mature adaptation to the prison.
privatization The process of involving private vendors and companies in the delivery of correctional services.
probation A sentence in which the offender resides in the community under general and specific conditions.
probationer An offender kept in the community, placed under certain restrictions, and monitored by a community corrections agent, typically referred to as a probation agent.
procedural due process The administration of justice according to established rules and principles; based on the principle that a person cannot be deprived of life, liberty, or property without appropriate legal procedures and safeguards.
professional override A probation officer's decision to overturn the outcome of a risk/needs assessment when determining the amount of supervision a probationer needs.
Progressive era The time period (late 1800s and early 1900s) in which advocates sought to bring science to corrections, focusing on individual casework.
proximate policy makers Elected officials—such as legislatures, prosecutors, and judges—who make decisions regarding probation departments' operations.
public agenda A perspective among correctional officers that promotes toughness and is punishment oriented toward prisoners.
public culture Beliefs held by prisoners that manipulation and use of force are the best strategies to adjust to the prison.
rabble management The role of the contemporary jail as defined by both society and the criminal justice system as a place to warehouse offenders who pose a threat to the social order largely due to their offensiveness, not their criminality.
reentry The process of an offender leaving prison and returning to a community.
reformatory model Initiated during the late 1800s, this model made education and work training the cornerstones for changing youthful offenders.
rehabilitation Changing the offender's behavior from criminal to positive and law abiding.
rehabilitative ideal The related concepts that offenders could be diagnosed, the problems leading to their criminality could be determined, programs or interventions to resolve those problems could be implemented, and the offenders could become law-abiding citizens.
reintegration The use of the community as a resource to instill law-abiding behavior in offenders.
responsibility model A model of prison management that stresses cooperation among inmates and prison officials in order to instill positive and responsible behaviors among prisoners.
restitution Money, work, or a service that offenders provide to the community as repayment for their crimes.
restoration Creating a balance of the needs of the offender, the victim, and the community as a way to promote law-abiding behavior.
restorative justice Programs and efforts centered on repairing the harm a crime has caused victims and the community. Can also refer to restoring an offender to the community, as the offender takes responsibility for the crime and undertakes personal change to become a law-abiding and acceptable member of society.
retribution An aim of punishment as the primary response to criminal wrongdoing.
revocation A judicial process used to rescind a court's probation order in the event a probationer fails to comply with the conditions of probation.
risk/needs assessment An instrument that helps probation officers determine the appropriate level of supervision a probationer requires.
sentenced offenders Persons sent to jail, usually for no more than 1 year, as determined by a court.
sentencing disparity A dilemma in which similar offenders receive different sentences.
separate system A penitentiary system developed in Pennsylvania that isolated each prisoner in a single cell, where they worked, ate, slept, and prayed alone.
shock probation A period of incarceration, after which the offender is re-sentenced to a period of probation.
specialized caseloads A caseload made up of offenders who share common characteristics or offenses (e.g., sex offenders).
specific conditions of probation Conditions unique to an offender that relate to his or her sentence.
split sentence A single sentence that involves both incarceration and community placement/probation.
standardization Consistency in operations resulting from the use of rules and procedures for recurring situations or aspects of operation.
street time Credit given to offenders whose probation is revoked to reduce their final time spent incarcerated after the revocation.
study release A program that allows inmates to enter the community to participate in academic or vocational education programs. Inmates reside in their designated facility, leave to study in the community for a specified period of the day, then return to their place of incarceration.
substantive due process The requirement that laws and regulations be related to a legitimate government interest and that resulting actions are fair and related to that interest.
sunk costs Financial commitments that cannot be easily reversed or abandoned.
super-max prisons Facilities designed to deal with the most dangerous of prisoners by preventing any interaction among inmates and restricting their physical movement.
term of supervised release (TSR) A form of release from prison; the law authorizes a sentencing judge to impose both a term of incarceration and a term of supervision in the community.
thief subculture The group of prisoners who hold the values of thievery and crime.
third-level alternatives Alternatives outside the normal range of sentencing options, such as prisons and probation, including electronic monitoring and house arrest.
underprediction A situation in which a decision is made that allows parole for those who might engage in a violent act according to the prediction scheme.
utilitarianism A philosophy emphasizing that humans are rational and will choose positive or pleasurable consequences over negative or painful ones and that the purpose of punishment should be desirable social aims, such as deterrence.
variables of interest Variables that are relevant to effectively supervising a probationer.
Walnut Street Jail The first jail in America, built in Philadelphia in 1790.
work release A program that allows inmates to work in a community setting. Inmates reside in their designated facility, leave to work in the community for a specified period of the day, then return to their place of incarceration.
