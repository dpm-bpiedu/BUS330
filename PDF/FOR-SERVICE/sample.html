balance on goods and services

business inventories

consumer durables
Goods that last, on average, a substantial length of time.
consumer nondurables

consumer services
That part of household consumption composed of nontangible activities.
consumption expenditures

Glossary

accommodative monetary policy Monetary expansion that is undertaken to help the treasury finance deficits without driving interest rates up too much.

Accord The 1951 agreement between the Fed and the treasury that the Fed was no longer obliged to hold interest rates low in order to assist with the treasury’s debt financing.

adaptive expectations Expectations about the future that are formed by economic actors on the basis of current and recent past experience and that adjust slowly to changing conditions.

aggregate demand The combined set of all goods and services produced in an economy and the average prices of those goods and services.

aggregate demand (AD) curve A graph showing the amounts of total real output that all buyers in an economy wish to purchase at various price levels.

aggregate expenditure (AE) Total planned spending for an economy’s total output.

aggregate expenditure function The sum of the two sources of planned spending: investment and consumption.

aggregate supply The total amount of goods and services that firms are willing to sell at a given price level during a specific time period in an economy.

aggregate supply (AS) curve A graph showing the amounts of total real output that all producers in an economy will offer for sale at various price levels.

allocation Any activities by a government or its agents that affect the distribution of resources and the combination of goods and services produced.

asset demand for money Demand for money to hold in order to protect the value of one’s assets against changes in interest rates. The asset demand for money is negatively related to the interest rate.

assumption The assumption that everything else will remain constant, used for most economic models. (Ceteris paribus is Latin for “all else being equal.”)

automatic stabilizers Changes in tax collections and transfer payments that are automatically triggered by changes in national income and tend to reduce changes in output and employment.

autonomous Pertaining to any variable that is not dependent on income.

balance on goods and services The difference between the value of exports and the value of imports for a country in any given year.

bank A financial institution that receives deposits.

bank panics Sudden waves of fear that banks will not be able to pay off their depositors.

barter Direct exchange of one good or service for another without the use of money.

behavioral economics A branch of economics that studies the effects of social, cognitive, and emotional components on the economic decision making of individuals and institutions.

big push strategy A development strategy calling for a major thrust on all fronts with government leadership and public–private partnership.

Board of Governors The central governing body of the Federal Reserve System.

business cycle Fluctuations in economic activity measured by the ups and downs in real output, employment, and prices.

business inventories Stocks of goods held by businesses from which they can make sales to meet demand.

capital The durable inputs into the production process created by people. Machines, tools, and buildings are examples of capital.

checkable deposits Balances in accounts at depository institutions against which checks can be drawn.

circular flow model A visual representation of the relationships between the resource market (in which income is obtained) and the product market (in which income is used to purchase goods and services).

classical aggregate supply curve The classical aggregate supply curve, with the economy producing at capacity (or full-employment level of output, Y*), is a vertical line.

command economy An economy in which the three basic questions are answered through central planning and control (also called a planned economy).
